30:30:40 split | Estate Settlement

All scenarios, stepwise math, property allocation, and explicit payment flows. Dec 2025

Audit Integrity Note

  • Assumptions: All property values, loan amounts, and escrow are as stated and fixed for this report. Only the estate-held 50% of Natasha is included in all calculations; Son1’s non-estate 50% is excluded. Internal settlement stress (20%) is applied only to internal scenarios, never to open-market sale math. Loan is treated as a neutral, non-profit, inflation-adjusted credit to Son2, not split or stressed. Escrow is always deducted at the estate level before any distribution. All calculations are deterministic, stepwise, and auditable.
  • Modeling limits: No speculative appreciation, no interest on loans, and all “drag” (tax, discount) is modeled as a direct deduction. Elder-care uplift is implemented as a one-time allocation, never double-counted. Any unused escrow is assumed to be redistributed per the estate split.
  • Intentionally conservative: This model intentionally errs on the side of conservatism to avoid overstating any party’s entitlement or estate value.

0. Executive Summary

This analysis evaluates a complex family estate settlement using a deterministic, audit-grade model. The core scenario is a 30:30:40 split of the estate among the three sons, with all calculations explicit, stepwise, and fully auditable. The model maximizes total family value, eliminates circular flows, and ensures all entitlements, loans, and elder-care adjustments are handled transparently and only once. All USD figures are disclosure equivalents only; all settlement math is denominated in INR.

Key Finding: Direct property allocation with ledger-based equalisation payments preserves estate value, avoids forced sales, and provides a transparent, conflict-minimizing settlement. The model ensures that all entitlements, loans, and elder-care adjustments are handled once, with no hidden estate cash pool or double-counting. Asset fragmentation and forced-sale penalties are explicitly modeled, and all scenarios are compared against open-market sale outcomes under stress.

  • Soft Stress Sale (10% discount + CGT): Post-tax proceeds: ₹8.0595 cr (~$899,500); Total loss: ₹1.99 cr (20%)
  • Moderate Stress Sale (20% discount + CGT): Post-tax proceeds: ₹7.164 cr (~$799,600); Total loss: ₹2.985 cr (30%)
  • High Stress / Forced Sale (30% discount + CGT): Post-tax proceeds: ₹6.2685 cr (~$699,400); Total loss: ₹3.98 cr (40%)

Leakage explanation: Sale % effect = discount + tax (sequential leakage). Internal % effect = stress on assets only; loan + escrow are neutral redistributions.

  • Gross Estate (stressed): ₹7.96 cr

4. Asset Economics: 506 Separation Penalty

4.1 Break-up Cost Rules (LOCKED)

  • Kitchen remediation cost: If 505+506 is broken up, a mandatory ₹0.30 cr (₹30 lakh) remediation is required for an independent kitchen build-out. This is paid only by the son who acquires the separated unit and reduces that acquirer’s effective entitlement.
  • Fire-sale penalty: If either flat is liquidated or separately marketed after breakup, apply a mandatory additional 10% discount to that flat’s applicable stressed value. This is applied after the applicable stress/discount already used for that context. The economic hit is borne by the party choosing/causing separate liquidation, not the estate.

4.2 Adjacency Constraint (MANDATORY)

  • Son1 & Son2 cannot live next door (505 vs 506).
  • Son1 & Son3 cannot live next door (505 vs 506).
  • Any breakup scenario where Son1 takes 505 and Son2 or Son3 takes 506 (or vice versa) is invalid.

4.3 Mandatory Conclusion

Breaking up Flats 505 and 506 introduces irreversible capital loss via mandatory remediation (₹0.30 cr) plus additional fire-sale discount (10% on post-stress value). These costs are borne by the party choosing/causing separation and are not neutral estate adjustments. Combined ownership remains the dominant structure unless one party explicitly absorbs the breakup penalty.
