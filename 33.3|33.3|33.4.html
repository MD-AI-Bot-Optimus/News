Rounding-adjusted equal split with audit-grade governance Jan 2026

0. Executive Summary

This page presents an internal family settlement under a rounding-adjusted equal split (33.3% / 33.3% / 33.4%), with audit-grade math, estate-level escrow handling, and Son2’s creditor loan credited outside the split. Internal values apply a locked 20% stress to estate assets only; sale math is handled separately.

At-a-glance

  • Q1. What is the stressed estate pool (assets only)?
    The total value of estate assets after applying a 20% internal stress, which is ₹7.960 cr.
  • Q2. What is the escrow holdback (estate-level)?
    A fixed amount of ₹0.050 cr set aside from the estate before distribution, used for final expenses or contingencies.
  • Q3. How is the loan credited to Son2 handled?
    Son2 receives a creditor repayment of ₹0.535 cr, which is not split or stressed, and is added to his entitlement.
  • Q4. What is the net remainder after escrow and loan?
    The distributable estate after deducting escrow and loan, totaling ₹7.375 cr.

Entitlements on Net Remainder

  • Son1: 0.333 × 7.375 = ₹2.4559 cr
  • Son2: 0.333 × 7.375 = ₹2.4559 cr; + Loan ₹0.535 ⇒ ₹2.9909 cr
  • Son3: 0.334 × 7.375 = ₹2.4633 cr
  • Check: ₹2.4559 + ₹2.9909 + ₹2.4633 = ₹7.910 cr ≙ (pool − escrow)

Governance locks

  • Escrow is an estate-level holdback, deducted before entitlements; no per-son escrow payments.
  • Loan is a creditor claim to Son2 (₹0.535 cr), not split and not stressed; added to Son2’s share.
  • Only the estate-owned 50% of Natasha is included (stressed ₹0.60 cr). Son1’s outside 50% is non-estate.
  • Diagrams render deterministically via Mermaid ESM (post-DOM load).

Scenarios below allocate 505+506 (kept combined by default), Arshee, and the estate’s 50% of Natasha, then compute equalisation payments to land precisely on the entitlements. Sale comparisons and separation penalties are addressed in dedicated sections.

1. Locked Parameters

1.1 Split Rule

  • Q5. What is Son1's share of the net estate?
    Son1 is entitled to 33.3% of the net estate remainder.
  • Q6. What is Son2's share of the net estate?
    Son2 is entitled to 33.3% of the net estate remainder, plus the loan credit.
  • Q7. What is Son3's share of the net estate?
    Son3 is entitled to 33.4% of the net estate remainder.

1.2 Governance

  • Q8. How is the net estate calculated?
    Net Estate = Stressed Gross Estate − Escrow − Loan.
  • Q9. How are entitlements determined?
    Son1 and Son2 each receive 33.3% of the net remainder (Son2 also gets the loan), and Son3 receives 33.4%.

1.3 Stress & Sale Separation

  • Q10. What is the internal stress applied to estate assets?
    A 20% reduction (×0.80) is applied to estate asset values for internal settlement calculations.
  • Q11. Are sale scenarios stressed?
    No, sale scenarios use market values and apply discounts and taxes, not the 20% internal stress.
  • Q12. How is escrow handled?
    Escrow is deducted at the estate level before entitlements; no individual son pays escrow directly.
  • Q13. How is the loan to Son2 treated?
    The loan is repaid to Son2 as a creditor claim, not split or stressed, and is added to his total entitlement.

4. Asset Economics: 506 Separation Penalty

  • Q37. What is the 506 separation penalty and when does it apply?
    The 506 separation penalty is a mandatory cost (₹0.30 cr for kitchen remediation plus a 10% fire-sale penalty on the separated unit) that applies if 505 and 506 are not held together. It is triggered when a son elects to take 506 (or 505) individually, breaking up the combined block and destroying the premium value.
  • Q38. Who bears the cost of remediation and fire-sale penalties?
    The son who causes the separation by taking a single unit (505 or 506) bears the full cost of remediation and any fire-sale penalty. These costs are not shared by the estate or other sons.
  • Q39. What is the adjacency constraint and why is it important?
    The adjacency constraint prohibits Son1 from living next door to Son2 or Son3 (i.e., 505 vs 506). Any scenario violating this is infeasible and excluded to avoid future conflict and maintain family harmony.
  • Q40. Why is combined ownership of 505+506 preferred?
    Combined ownership preserves the premium value of the block and avoids mandatory penalties. Breaking up the block results in irreversible capital loss, making combined ownership the dominant economic strategy.

Cost‑bearing guidance

  • Remediation and fire‑sale penalties are borne by the party choosing/causing separation; not by the estate.
  • Adjacency constraint: scenarios where Son1 lives next door to Son2 or Son3 (505 vs 506) are infeasible and excluded.

Conclusion: Combined ownership is economically dominant unless one party explicitly absorbs the separation penalty.

Scenario 3: Forced Sale (High Stress: 30% discount + CGT)

  • Q50. What triggers a forced sale in this model?
    A forced sale is triggered when the estate must liquidate all assets under distress, typically due to lack of agreement or urgent need for cash. This results in deep discounts and tax drag, eroding estate value.
  • Q51. How are sale proceeds distributed after deductions?
    After applying the 30% discount and 10% CGT, escrow and the loan are carved out at the estate level. The remainder is split 33.3/33.3/33.4, with Son2 receiving the loan repayment in addition to his share.
  • Q52. What is the overall percentage effect of a forced sale?
    The overall effect is a 40% reduction in estate value compared to the gross base, due to sequential leakage from discount and tax.

Sale math uses locked values and does not apply the internal 20% stress. Accounting order: Gross sale → CGT → escrow carve‑out → loan repayment to Son2 → remainder split 33.3/33.3/33.4; add loan to Son2’s total.
