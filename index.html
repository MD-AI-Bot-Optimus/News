3. Legal Context and Administrative Background

The elimination of the automatic EAD extension was a calculated move by the Trump administration that immediately followed a major legal victory for immigrant advocates, creating a swift and targeted policy reversal. This sequence of events maximizes the impact on certain employment-authorized foreign nationals, particularly those in the H-4 and Adjustment of Status (AOS) categories, to prioritize American jobs. (DHS Action Timeline, Nov 2025). Verified - Likelihood: 100%

  • Authority used: DHS invoked the “good-cause” exemption under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA §553(b)(3)(B)) to issue the IFR without prior notice-and-comment. (APA §553(b)(3)(B)). Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • Policy rationale: To prioritize proper vetting and restore integrity to work-authorization processing. (DHS Rationale, Oct 29, 2025). Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • Litigation posture: AILA filed suit (Nov 3, 2025) alleging arbitrary rulemaking; additional class actions are anticipated. (AILA v. DHS, Case 1:25-cv-03211, D.D.C.). Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • Public input: Early docket comments are trending toward tens of thousands before the Dec 1 deadline. (Federal Register Docket, Nov 2025). Verified - Likelihood: 85%

1. The H-4 EAD Program Was Upheld (October 14, 2025)

The decade-long legal challenge to the H-4 EAD program—the lawsuit Save Jobs USA v. DHS—effectively ended on October 14, 2025. Verified - Likelihood: 100%

  • The Lawsuit’s Goal: Opponents of the H-4 EAD rule sought to have the program declared illegal, arguing that DHS exceeded its statutory authority in allowing spouses of H-1B holders (who are awaiting green cards) to work. Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • The Outcome: The Supreme Court denied certiorari (refused to hear the case). Verified - Likelihood: 100% This left the ruling of the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals intact, which had upheld the legality of the H-4 EAD program. Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • The Optics: The denial was widely seen as a victory for immigrant families and a loss for groups aligned with the “America First” policy goals of limiting foreign work authorization. Verified - Likelihood: 95%

2. The Executive Action Takes Aim (October 29, 2025)

Just two weeks after the Supreme Court’s denial, the administration initiated an immediate, broad-based policy counter-move:

  • The Action: DHS published an Interim Final Rule (IFR) on October 29, 2025, which took effect on October 30, 2025. Verified - Likelihood: 100% This IFR eliminated the 540-day automatic extension for most EAD renewal applications, including those held by H-4 spouses (category C26) and AOS applicants (category C09). Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • The Mechanism: By using the IFR process, DHS invoked a “good cause” exemption under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), bypassing the normal notice-and-comment period. The official justification was to “prioritize proper screening and vetting” and eliminate policies that prioritized “aliens’ convenience ahead of Americans’ safety and security.” Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • The Effect: While the Supreme Court confirmed the legality of the H-4 EAD program, the DHS action effectively cripples the usability of the EAD for over a million people. By removing the 540-day buffer, EAD holders now face guaranteed job loss if their renewal takes longer than their current card’s expiration date—a near certainty given the long-standing USCIS backlogs. Verified - Likelihood: 95%

4. Layoff Waves

The EAD layoff waves are a projected outcome of the Department of Homeland Security’s (DHS) Interim Final Rule (IFR), effective October 30, 2025, which eliminated the 540-day automatic extension for Employment Authorization Documents (EADs) for certain categories of workers. Verified - Likelihood: 100% This policy change essentially sets a cliff date for many high-"skilled workers" who rely on the EAD to prove their authorization to work in the U.S.

4.1 The Early Wave: Q1/Q2 2026 (The Bulk of Layoffs)

This wave is the most disruptive and affects the vast majority of current EAD renewal applicants who will file after the October 30, 2025, cutoff.

  • Affected Population: Approximately 1.255 million EAD holders (including H-4 spouses, pending Adjustment of Status (AOS) applicants (C09), and certain others) who filed their renewals on or after October 30, 2025. Speculative - Likelihood: 90%
  • The Problem: EAD renewal processing times at USCIS often range from 6 to 12 months (and sometimes longer). Verified - Likelihood: 95% Under the new rule, these workers lose work authorization the instant their current EAD card expires. Verified - Likelihood: 95%
  • The Cliff: Since most EAD renewal applications can only be filed up to 180 days (6 months) before the card expires, many workers whose cards expire in April, May, or June 2026 will not have their new EAD card in hand in time. Verified - Likelihood: 95%
  • Corporate Action: Employers must comply with I-9 verification rules and cannot allow an employee to work without valid authorization. Verified - Likelihood: 100% To protect against I-9 fines (up to $28,619 per violation) and to manage corporate compliance, companies are expected to issue termination notices 60 to 60 to 90 days before the EAD expiration date. This front-loads the job loss into February to March 2026, leading to the predicted “Wave 1” job losses. Verified - Likelihood: 85%

4.2 The Delayed Wave: Late 2027

This smaller wave consists of those who filed their renewal applications before the IFR took effect.

  • Affected Population: A small fraction (~3.5% or ~45,000) of workers who filed their EAD renewal applications on or before October 29, 2025. Speculative - Likelihood: 85%
  • The Exception: These individuals retain the original 540-day automatic extension (about 18 months) while their renewal is processed. This gives them a lengthy grace period. Verified - Likelihood: 95%
  • The Cliff: Their work authorization will finally terminate around November–December 2027 (540 days after their 2026 card expiration) if their renewal has still not been processed, leading to a much smaller, delayed “Wave 2.” Verified - Likelihood: 85%

  • Strategic Timing: The Supreme Court’s refusal to hear Save Jobs USA v. DHS on October 14, 2025, left the H-4 EAD program legally intact. Verified - Likelihood: 100% Rather than accept this as a final defeat, the administration used the IFR to bypass the courts entirely. Verified - Likelihood: 100%
  • Policy Substitution: While the core H-4 EAD program remains lawful, removing the 540-day buffer renders it practically unusable for over a million people due to USCIS backlogs. Verified - Likelihood: 95% This achieves the same end goal—reducing foreign labor participation—without overturning the program itself. Verified - Likelihood: 95%
  • Broader Scope: The IFR goes beyond H-4 EADs and eliminates the 540-day extension for all EAD categories that previously qualified, including AOS (C09), TPS, and OPT/STEM—effectively “rounding up” a much larger population than just H-4 spouses. Verified - Likelihood: 95%

11. Legal Status After EAD Expiration for Pending Green Card Applicants

Individuals with a pending Form I-485 (Application to Adjust Status) for a green card can legally remain in the U.S. even after their EAD expires, as the pending adjustment of status maintains their lawful presence during processing. (USCIS Green Card Processes, Oct 30, 2025; Baylor University International Services, pending I-485 status). Verified - Likelihood: 100% However, without a valid EAD, they are not authorized to work, and any employment could violate immigration laws, risking denial of their green card application or deportation. (USCIS Options for Nonimmigrant Workers Following Termination, Jan 24, 2025; Reddit r/h1b discussion, May 21, 2024). Verified - Likelihood: 100%

This status is known as a “period of authorized stay,” distinct from lawful permanent resident status, allowing them to stay but not work until the EAD is renewed or the green card is approved. (USCIS While Your Green Card Application Is Pending, Oct 30, 2025). Verified - Likelihood: 100% For those in categories like T or U nonimmigrant status with pending I-485, similar rules apply, but they must comply with specific conditions. (USCIS T and U Nonimmigrant Status, pending I-485 extension). Verified - Likelihood: 100%

(INA §274A; USCIS Employment Authorization Document). Verified - Likelihood: 100% This unauthorized work can also jeopardize their pending I-485, as it may be seen as a status violation. (The Anwari Law Firm, EAD and Pending I-485 Status). Verified - Likelihood: 100%
