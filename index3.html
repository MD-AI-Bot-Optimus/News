Estate Settlement — All Combinations

All scenarios, stepwise math, property allocation, and explicit payment flows. Dec 2025

0. Executive Summary

This analysis evaluates an estate settlement using a clean “Estate Pool + Ledger” model. Each property is allocated directly to a single son. All calculations are based on explicit property values, internal stress, and audit-grade loan and escrow treatment. Entitlements are determined by the ownership pattern, elder-care uplift, and loan credits.

Key Finding: Direct property allocation with ledger-based equalisation payments preserves Estate value, eliminates circular flows, and provides a transparent, conflict-minimizing settlement. The model ensures all entitlements, loans, and elder-care adjustments are handled once, with no hidden estate cash pool or double-counting.

1.2 Model Structure

  • Loan credit to Son2 is funded equally by Son1 and Son3 (Model B logic).
  • Escrow is paid directly by Son3.

3. Financial Comparison & Impact Analysis: Internal vs. External Settlement

This document is a financial comparison and impact analysis that argues for an Internal Settlement (keeping properties within the Estate) rather than selling them on the open market.

It calculates how much money the Estate "loses" to taxes and market conditions under different scenarios. Here is the breakdown of what this is saying:

3.1. The Core Argument: "Internal" vs. "External"

The document highlights that selling properties to outsiders is "catastrophic" for Estate wealth due to Leakage (money leaving the Estate).

  • Internal Settlement: The Estate voluntarily "stresses" (discounts) the value by 20% to make buying each other out easier. No actual tax is paid to the government; the value stays within the brothers.
  • Market Sale: You lose money twice—first to a Market Discount (buyers bargaining you down) and second to Capital Gains Tax (CGT) (paying the government).

3.2. The "Leakage" Comparison (Total Wealth Destruction)

This is the most critical part of the document. It shows the "Effective Loss" of the Estate's total wealth (starting from a base market value of ₹9.95 cr):

3.3. The Property Values (Stressed vs. Market)

To settle internally, the Estate uses "Stressed Values." This is the price one brother pays another to "buy" their share.

  • 505+506: Market ₹4.95 cr Settlement ₹3.96 cr
  • Arshee: Market ₹4.25 cr Settlement ₹3.40 cr
  • Natasha (50%): Market ₹0.75 cr Settlement ₹0.60 cr

3.4. Final Payouts to the Sons (Internal Settlement)

After accounting for a ₹0.535 cr loan owed to Son 2 and a ₹0.05 cr escrow (funeral/legal fund), the final "entitlements" are:

  • Son 1: ₹2.1025 cr
  • Son 2: ₹2.9050 cr
  • Son 3: ₹2.9525 cr

How These Numbers Are Calculated

  • Estate Pool (stressed): 505+506: ₹3.96cr, Arshee: ₹3.40cr, Natasha (estate 50%): ₹0.60cr. Total: ₹7.96cr
  • 505+506 split: ₹3.96cr ÷ 3 = ₹1.32cr each
  • Natasha (estate 50%) split: ₹0.60cr ÷ 3 = ₹0.20cr each
  • Arshee split: Son1: 25% × ₹3.40cr = ₹0.85cr; Son2: 25% × ₹3.40cr = ₹0.85cr; Son3: 50% × ₹3.40cr = ₹1.70cr (elder-care uplift included)
  • Baseline entitlements: Son1: ₹1.32 + ₹0.20 + ₹0.85 = ₹2.37cr; Son2: ₹1.32 + ₹0.20 + ₹0.85 = ₹2.37cr; Son3: ₹1.32 + ₹0.20 + ₹1.70 = ₹3.22cr
  • Loan adjustment (Model B): Son1: −₹0.2675cr; Son2: +₹0.5350cr; Son3: −₹0.2675cr
  • Final entitlements: Son1: ₹2.37 − ₹0.2675 = ₹2.1025cr; Son2: ₹2.37 + ₹0.5350 = ₹2.9050cr; Son3: ₹3.22 − ₹0.2675 = ₹2.9525cr

4. SECTION 4 — ASSET ECONOMICS: THE 506 SEPARATION PENALTY

We analyze the economic consequences if a son elects to take Flat 506 individually. This triggers a "Break-Up Penalty" because it destroys the combined value of the 505+506 block and forces a distressed liquidation of the remaining unit.

4.1 The "Acquirer-Pays" Principle

When the 505+506 block is split, the son choosing the individual unit (506) is responsible for the financial "leakage" caused by that decision. This ensures the other brothers are not penalized for the loss of the combined asset's premium.

4.2 Breakdown of Penalties (The "Break-Up Cost")

  • 1. Physical Remediation (Kitchen): ₹0.30 cr
    Reason: Flat 506 must be made a self-contained unit by adding/separating walls and plumbing from 505.
  • 2. Forced Sale Leakage on 505: ₹0.6375 cr
    Reason: By taking 506, the son forces the estate to sell 505 as a standalone studio under Forced Sale conditions.
    Calculation: Market Value of 505 (₹1.70 cr) × 37.5% (Market Discount + CGT).
  • 3. Total Separation Penalty: ₹0.9375 cr

4.3 Hypothetical Entitlement Impact

The table below shows how choosing Flat 506 impacts the son’s "Net Position" compared to taking the Combined Block or receiving Cash/Natasha.

ItemOption A: Take Combined BlockOption B: Take 506 Individually
Asset Transferred505+506 (Block)Flat 506 (Standalone)
Stressed Asset Value₹3.96 cr₹2.60 cr
Kitchen Remediation₹0.00(₹0.30 cr)
Forced Sale Loss on 505₹0.00(₹0.6375 cr)
Net Value Charged to Son₹3.96 cr₹3.5375 cr

4.4 Strategic Conclusion

  • The "Luxury Gap": By taking only 506, the son receives an asset worth only ₹2.60 cr, but is "charged" ₹3.5375 cr against their entitlement due to the penalties.
  • Estate Protection: This math protects the estate pool, ensuring that the ₹0.9375 cr lost to construction and taxes is "pre-paid" by the son who insisted on the split.
  • Recommendation: It is mathematically superior for a son to either take the Entire Block (₹3.96 cr) or Natasha/Cash, as the standalone 506 option carries a 36% "inefficiency tax" borne entirely by the acquirer.
