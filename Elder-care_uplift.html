3. Financial Comparison & Impact Analysis: Internal vs. External Settlement

Possible clarifying questions
  • Q31. How does internal settlement compare to an open-market sale?
    Internal settlement preserves more estate value by avoiding market discounts and taxes. Open-market sales result in significant value leakage due to forced discounts and tax drag, while internal settlement uses a 20% stress but keeps value within the family.
  • Q32. What are the different sale scenarios modeled in this section?
    The scenarios include soft sale (10% discount), moderate sale (20% discount), and forced sale (30% discount), each with an additional tax drag proxy. These illustrate the range of possible outcomes if the estate is liquidated externally.
  • Q33. How are sale discounts and capital gains tax applied in the calculations?
    Sale discounts are applied first to the gross estate value, followed by a tax drag proxy (e.g., 10%). The result is a sequential reduction in distributable proceeds, modeling real-world sale leakage.
  • Q34. What is the “sequential leakage” mentioned in the sale scenarios?
    Sequential leakage refers to the compounding effect of applying a market discount and then a tax drag, which together reduce the estate’s net proceeds more than either would alone.
  • Q35. How does the model ensure that loan and escrow are treated neutrally in sale math?
    Loan and escrow are carved out from the post-tax proceeds before distribution. The loan is repaid directly to Son2, and escrow is held at the estate level, ensuring neither is double-counted or unfairly allocated.
  • Q36. What is the overall percentage effect of each sale scenario?
    Soft sale: 20% loss, moderate sale: 30% loss, forced sale: 40% loss versus gross estate value. These percentages reflect the combined impact of discounts and taxes.
  • Q37. How does the model handle partial sales versus total liquidation?
    Partial sales are modeled by selling only some assets (e.g., 505+506) and distributing proceeds accordingly, while the rest are retained. The model tracks value leakage and entitlements in both cases.
  • Q38. What are the main value leakages in forced sale scenarios?
    The main leakages are deep market discounts (up to 30%) and tax drag, which together can erode up to 40% of the estate’s value. Forced sales also risk suboptimal timing and distressed pricing.
  • Q39. How are the proceeds split among the sons in each sale scenario?
    After deducting loan and escrow, the remainder is split according to the model’s rules (e.g., 30:30:40 or as per Arshee uplift), with Son2 receiving the loan repayment directly.
  • Q40. Can you explain the difference between “soft,” “moderate,” and “forced” sale scenarios?
    A soft sale assumes a 10% discount, moderate sale 20%, and forced sale 30%, each with a tax drag. The higher the discount, the greater the value loss, modeling increasing urgency or distress in the sale process.
